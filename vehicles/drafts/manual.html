Overview

Scope. This manual is the HVAC-specific layer of driver training. It covers our vehicles, local operations and procedures, and the on-the-job competencies a driver is signed off on. It does not teach the general theory and skills of safe emergency-vehicle operation – defensive driving, the law, intersection negotiation, and vehicle dynamics – which are the subject of the national Emergency Vehicle Operator Course (EVOC). HVAC does not require EVOC, but recommends it as a foundation; see the companion EVOC reference guide for an overview of those topics.

First and foremost, this isn't just a training manual for drivers – it is intended as a reference for all HVAC members, since everyone should know the basics about the vehicles they ride in. Awareness is key. This manual is divided into three modules:

  1. Introduction to Vehicles: required for all members.
  2. Introduction to Emergency Vehicles: required for all members with a driver's license.
  3. Emergency Vehicle Operations: required for all driver trainees.

As members get closer to driver training, they progress through the modules. This structure is necessary because non-driver members may be called upon to drive other vehicles, such as an ambulance or the supervisor's vehicle, in urgent situations. All members with a driver's license should be, at minimum, capable of operating emergency vehicles in a non-emergency mode safely and expeditiously.

Responsibilities and goals. As a driver, you are responsible for the safety of the crew, the patient, and others, including family members, police officers, and paramedics. A previous Vehicles Department adopted the motto "Slow is smooth, smooth is fast." Keep that in mind, with the goal of getting through the shift safely while supporting excellent patient care. Before scheduling road training with an FTO, you should know where each piece of equipment is located across the different ambulances in the fleet.

Section I: Introduction to Vehicles All members

Truck Familiarization

The fleet includes two ambulance body styles. The Type III units are "box" ambulances, with a modular patient box on a cutaway van chassis; they weigh on the order of 14,000 lbs, and the rear box creates an overhang that swings during turns (see Overhang and blind spots). The Type II units are van ambulances, with an integrated raised-roof van body rather than a separate box; they are lighter and tend to be more maneuverable. Members should be able to identify each rig; learning each rig's supplies, equipment locations, and controls is the trainee's responsibility, as described below.

Controls and consoles vary by rig

The rigs are not identical to operate. Controls differ from truck to truck: for example, 922 uses electronic push-buttons where other trucks use manual switches, the light and siren controls differ slightly between units, and the built-in radios are not the same across the fleet. A driver must be able to operate each rig's controls confidently. Demonstrating competent operation of each rig is part of advancing from Level 1 to Level 2.

General Driving Policies

  • Follow the rig rotation on the whiteboard in the garage.
  • Perform a rig check, and report any issues to the Vehicles Department.
  • Carry a D-1 radio.
  • Park ambulances inside and on a shoreline when not in use.
  • Use a spotter when backing up.
  • When leaving the city to get gas, the crew chief informs HPD.
  • Make sure the vehicle is clean at the end of the shift.
  • No open food or drink in the vehicle, and no vaping or smoking.
  • No loose items on the dashboard, such as glove boxes or food.
  • Follow traffic rules, especially when using lights and sirens.
  • Report any damage or incidents.
Most rigs are state certified. If an inspector sees loose items on the dashboard of a certified rig, that is an example of a finable offense. Keep the rig clean and items secured.

Policies: Lights and Sirens, Other Vehicles

Lights and sirens assist navigation through traffic, but they do not change the laws that apply to the driver (covered in Section II). The supervisor's vehicle (Charlie, also called the flycar) is an SUV used for response only, not patient transport, and may be operated by members with a valid driver's license in urgent situations. It handles differently from a box ambulance.

Section II: Introduction to Emergency Vehicles Members with a driver's license

Definitions: The Law

Important reminder: HVAC must follow the same laws as any civilian vehicle. We do not have the exemptions that FD or PD enjoy. Lights and sirens are not a waiver of liability. You may be held responsible for an accident you cause while responding or transporting. You are in charge of the vehicle; if you judge it unsafe to proceed through a crowded intersection, do not proceed.

So:

  • NO SPEEDING.
  • NO "wrong way" driving.
  • DO NOT pass a school bus when its stop sign and flashing red lights are activated.
  • DO NOT cross railroad tracks when the red light is activated.

The vast majority of calls are within a few miles of the hospital. Higher speed may shave a few minutes off a trip, but it greatly increases the possibility of an accident. A relevant data summary is worth reading: NHTSA data on ground ambulance crashes.

Response Types

Hoboken dispatch does not use response codes, but sometimes uses phrases that imply the response type. For example, an instruction to "check one out" at a location is typically a non-urgent response. An urgent response uses lights and sirens; a non-urgent response does not. A quick conversation with your crew chief can confirm the response type, and it may change en route, so be ready for that.

Sterile Response

During a response, limit unrelated conversation, and be a good passenger so the driver can concentrate.

Warning Devices: Appropriate Use

  • Why? EMTs are expected to determine whether the patient needs a lights-and-sirens transport. Drivers are expected to assess environmental and operational conditions, such as weather, roads, traffic, and distance, and to determine whether honoring the EMT's directive would be safe.
Siren operation. Drivers stopped ahead of you at a red light are not equipped with the same visual or audible warnings you have, so do not force them through an intersection by blaring your siren, as it is unsafe for them to proceed. Stay collected and professional; displaying anger will not change the situation and only raises everyone's stress level. If an incident results, you may be held responsible.

Section III: Emergency Vehicle Operations Driver trainees

Driver trainee progression

Driver training is a graduated progression through three levels of increasing independence. To enter Level 1, a trainee must have submitted and had reviewed all paperwork (driver's license and abstract), hold a valid current driver's license, passed the quiz on this manual and the EVOC reference guide, and been cleared on the rig check and on the location of all ambulance contents.

Level Permits Supervision Advancement
Level 1 Non-emergent driving (no lights or sirens) FTO in the front seat After a minimum of three shifts, any FTO may clear the trainee to Level 2
Level 2 Emergent driving (lights and sirens) FTO in the front seat After a minimum of three shifts, any FTO may clear the trainee to Level 3
Level 3 Emergent driving (lights and sirens) FTO anywhere in the ambulance After a minimum of three shifts and a recommendation from two FTOs, the Vehicles Lt or Sgt may clear the trainee to full driver status

The per-level skill checks, the quizzes, and the FTO sign-off records are maintained in the separate Driver Trainee Task Book. This overview covers the gating logic only; the task book is the single source for the checks and criteria.

The following are the competency tasks an FTO signs off on across the levels. They follow the arc of a shift, from preparation through return to base.

Operational note – Overhang and blind spots. On a Type III box, the overhang is the part of the vehicle from the rear axle (the pivot point) to the rear bumper. On a turn, everything ahead of the rear axle moves in the direction you steer, and the overhang swings the opposite way. On a left turn, watch the passenger-side rear corner; on a right turn, watch the driver-side rear corner. Make sure no one is in that area before turning, and always check mirrors before changing lane, direction, or pace.

Vehicle Contact and Accidents

This applies to every driver, not only to trainees. Any contact involving a corps vehicle is reported, however minor – a clipped mirror, a backing scrape, or contact with a fixed object – with no exceptions for damage that looks trivial.

At the scene

Is anyone injured? Call HPD right away, give your location, and request additional resources if necessary; treat injuries. If there is no injury, the vehicle is safe to operate, and you were responding or transporting, take information and photos of the damage, then advise HPD that you are resuming. If there are injuries, the vehicle is unsafe, or you were not in response or transport, remain on scene. If the vehicle needs to be towed, call the Vehicles Officers, the 1st Lieutenant, or the Captain. When back available or at the end of the shift, every member of the crew is to complete an incident report form.

Re-clearance after an incident

Any at-fault or significant vehicle contact suspends the driver's status pending review. Return to driving requires re-clearance by the Vehicles Lt or Sgt, which may include remediation or a drop to a supervised level (a return to Level 2 or Level 1 under an FTO). The corps does not run an annual driver renewal; incident-driven review is the mechanism for confirming continued fitness to drive.
