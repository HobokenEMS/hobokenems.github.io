How to use this guide. This is a short, plain-language reference to the safe-driving principles taught in the national EVOC curriculum. HVAC does not require EVOC, but recommends it, and these principles apply to every member who drives a corps vehicle. For our vehicles, codes, and procedures, use the HVAC Driver Training Manual instead; this guide stays general on purpose.

1. Due regard and the law

An emergency vehicle operator is held to a standard of due regard for the safety of everyone on the road. Lights and sirens request the right of way; they do not grant it, and they do not exempt the driver from responsibility. If an operator drives without due regard and causes harm, the operator can be held liable regardless of the emergency. The practical rule is that no call justifies a collision.

2. Defensive driving and the cushion of safety

Defensive driving means anticipating the mistakes of others and leaving yourself room and time to react. Three habits carry most of the benefit:

  • Scan ahead. Keep your eyes moving and look well down the road, not just at the vehicle in front of you, so hazards are seen early.
  • Keep a cushion of safety. Maintain open space on all sides of the vehicle, especially in front, so you always have somewhere to go and time to stop.
  • Communicate. Use signals, position, and timing so other drivers understand your intentions and are not surprised.

3. Speed and stopping distance

Stopping distance grows faster than speed: a modest increase in speed produces a large increase in the distance needed to stop, and it shortens the time available to react. On the short trips typical of an urban service, higher speed saves little time while sharply raising the risk and severity of a crash. Controlled, steady speed is both safer and, in traffic, usually no slower.

4. Intersections

Intersections are the single most dangerous place for an emergency vehicle, because they are where paths cross and where other drivers are least likely to see or hear you in time. Treat every intersection as a hazard:

  • Slow down, and come to a stop for a red light or stop sign.
  • Clear the intersection lane by lane, confirming each lane has yielded before proceeding.
  • Make eye contact with other drivers where possible.
  • Change the siren tone on approach, since a steady tone can be hard to locate.
  • Be ready for the driver who stops suddenly, or who does not stop at all.

5. Vehicle dynamics

An ambulance is heavy, tall, and long, and it behaves nothing like a personal vehicle. It needs more distance to stop, leans more in turns, and is less forgiving of sudden inputs. Two effects deserve constant attention:

  • Blind spots are large, particularly to the rear and sides; mirrors do not show everything.
  • Tail swing and overhang. The body behind the rear axle swings opposite the direction of a turn, so the rear can strike objects or people the driver has already passed.

6. Backing

Backing causes a large share of preventable emergency-vehicle collisions, almost all at low speed and out of the driver's sight. The standard protection is simple and not optional: back with a spotter, keep the spotter visible, and stop the moment the spotter leaves view. When no spotter is available, get out and look before backing.

7. Lights and sirens

Warning devices are a request for cooperation, not a guarantee of it. Modern vehicles are well insulated, drivers are distracted, and a siren may be heard only at the last moment. Use lights and sirens deliberately, expect that some drivers will not react as hoped, and never assume you have been seen. Avoid pushing a stopped driver through an intersection against a red light, which transfers your risk onto them.

8. Fatigue and distraction

Fatigue degrades judgment and reaction time in much the same way as impairment, and it is common on long or overnight shifts. In-cab distraction, including phones, screens, and conversation, competes for the attention the road needs. Keep the cab calm during a response, limit unrelated talk, and recognize when fatigue means someone else should drive.

9. Adverse conditions

Rain, snow, ice, darkness, and heavy traffic all reduce traction, visibility, or both. The response is consistent: reduce speed, increase following distance, brake earlier and more gently, and give yourself a wider margin everywhere. Conditions, not the dispatch, set the safe speed.

10. Occupant and scene safety

Safe operation continues after the vehicle stops:

  • Everyone is restrained whenever the vehicle is moving, and loose equipment is secured so it cannot become a projectile.
  • Position the vehicle on scene to protect the crew and the work area, and use it to shield against traffic where appropriate.
  • Wear high-visibility protection in or near the roadway, and stay alert to traffic, bicycles, and scooters when opening doors and stepping out.
Bottom line. The common thread across all ten topics is margin: time, space, and attention. Build margin in, and most emergency-driving hazards become manageable. For the corps-specific application of these ideas, return to the HVAC Driver Training Manual.
